What is a Mushroom?

A mushroom is the conspicuous umbrella-shaped fruiting body (sporophore) of certain fungi, typically of the order Agaricales in the phylum Basidiomycota but also of some other groups. Popularly, the term mushroom is used to identify the edible sporophores; the term toadstool is often reserved for inedible or poisonous sporophores. There is, however, no scientific distinction between the two names, and either can be properly applied to any fleshy fungus fruiting structure.

How Do We Classify Mushrooms?

Mushrooms can be classified as either edible or poisonous based on features like cap shape, gill type, odor, and habitat. Our machine learning model helps predict mushroom safety using these visible characteristics.

Types of Mushrooms

Amanita

Highly poisonous. Red cap with white spots. Avoid consumption!

Chanterelle

Edible and delicious. Yellow-orange. Used in gourmet cooking.

Death Cap

One of the deadliest mushrooms. Pale green. Extremely toxic.

Morel

Edible with a honeycomb texture. Prized by chefs worldwide.

Fly Agaric

Bright red with white dots. Poisonous and hallucinogenic.

Oyster Mushroom

Edible and popular in cooking. Mild flavor and soft texture.

The Fascinating World of Mushroom Farming and Edible Varieties

Mushrooms have captivated human interest for centuries, not only for their unique flavors, but also for their fascinating cultivation and health benefits.

A Journey Through Time

Mushroom farming began in ancient China over 1,000 years ago with shiitake mushrooms. In the 17th century, France pioneered cave-based mushroom growing. In the U.S., it began in 1896 and expanded rapidly with European influence and spawn technology.
